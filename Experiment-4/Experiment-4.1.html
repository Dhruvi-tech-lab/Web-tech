Rani Ki Vav

Rani Ki Vav


About the location: The only real sign of Patan’s former glory is this astoundingly beautiful stepwell. Located on the banks of the Saraswati River, this is one of the oldest and finest stepwells in Gujarat and is remarkably preserved. Steps lead down through multiple levels with lines of carved pillars and more than 800 sculptures, mostly on Vishnu-avatar themes, as well as striking geometric patterns. The stepwell is made in the form of an inverted temple. It showcases superior craftsmanship of the era, and the unique Maru-Gurjara architectural style and houses sculptures similar to the Vimalavasahi temple in Mount Abu and Sun temple at Modhera.

Brief History: The stepwell was built in 1063 by the Chaulukya Dynasty to commemorate her husband, Bhimdev. A Jain monk, Merutunga, mentions that Udayamati, the daughter of Naravaraha Khangara, built this stepwell at Patan. The same composition also states that the stepwell was commissioned in 1063 and took around 20 years to complete. Archaeologists Henry Cousens and James Burgess visited the site in the 1890s when it was completely buried under silt, with only a few pillars visible. The stepwell was rediscovered in the 1940s and restored in the 1980s. Since 2014, it has been recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site.


Location: Rani Ki Vav, Patan, Gujarat, India.

Source: UNESCO World Heritage Centre
